Guerilla Gardening

For several years, a group of Metrofarm members has been engaged in a very specific type of urban gardening – guerrilla gardening. During the Take Back Your Street event organized by the Automat Association, we planted an entire street. In the following article, we’ll talk you through guerilla gardening.

Try it too!

What is guerilla gardening?

Guerilla gardening is a form of urban gardening. Some see it as environmental activism, others as a new approach to urban space cultivation. Either way, thanks to guerilla gardening, a neglected and dirty urban place (or in a village or by the road) turns into a small flower bed.

This global trend has also found its followers in the Czech Republic. In many places, groups of guerrilla gardeners cultivate neglected patches under trees or abandoned large-capacity concrete planters (we all know them). They clean them, weed them, and quickly turn the once hopeless spaces into something beautiful and unusual. Guerilla gardening is also a popular teambuilding activity – creating something nice together that makes sense has a positive effect on a group of people. We at Metrofarm are no strangers to this.

How can you do it?

Ask first

If you decide to do something about a neglected public space, first find out how much the owner will mind your intervention. If they’ll mind a lot, don’t do it. Guerilla gardening, in our opinion, should not be a hate crime. In Prague, guerilla gardening on city land is informally tolerated, but it is always necessary to weigh the risks and find out if the flower bed does not belong to someone who will hate your intervention or whether your activities won’t cause more harm than good.

Prepare Your “Field”

If there are no major issues, this usually doesn’t take a lot of time. It takes just one day (or even a couple of hours). First, you need to clean the place from litter, excrements and weeds. Always use protective gloves for this. As you probably can imagine, it’s not always clean work. When the place is clean, add or change substrate.

Drive the Change

The next step is the most popular and photogenic – planting seedlings or sowing seeds. Kids love helping with this step and believe it or not, guerilla gardening with children is the best.

Make It Rain

Finally, you need to water the flower bed. Until the seeds or seedlings take root, you need to water them regularly. Adjust the size and number of the flower beds to your availability. The easiest way is scattering the seeds over the surface, covering them with soil, gently compressing it with your foot and letting the rain and nature do its magic. You’ll feel immense joy from each successful plant.

Further Care

Ideally, you should take care of the flower bed continuously. But it’s not necessary. If you plant close to your home, it’s not difficult to water it during major droughts or to weed out the worst weeds.

How to mentally cope with vandalism?

Many people are moved by the transformation of these spaces. Other neighbors will be inspired to start their own gardening activity – which is the primary goal of guerrilla gardening. But there are also those who do not share this joy with others. If you’re lucky, they’ll just ignore the flower beds but they can also destroy them. You have to take this into account. As well as any damage caused by animals and weather conditions. Life on the street is hard. Guerrilla gardeners often accept that the journey can also be the destination. And all the more they appreciate when sometimes the flower bed survive until the winter.

If you expect more stability and yields from your urban gardening, guerilla gardening is not the right way for you. At least not until the general opinion on the use of public space drastically changes. Rent a flower bed in a community garden instead (for example, in our Metrofarm), or try gardening on your balcony or window sill.

What else is good to know about guerilla gardening?

Is it legal?

Strictly speaking, guerilla gardening is illegal. Be prepared for this and do it only where the flower beds will cause joy, not problems.

Can I modify the soil around the trees in the street?

If you're working with flower patchesunder trees, never bury the tree trunk with soil – not even with a 10cm layer of dirt. It could kill the tree. You can put a fence around the flower bed, but it will create other problems, for example, with vandals. It is better to let the flower bed live its own life – and that includes any potential losses. That's what’s life on the street is about.

How to deal with dogs visiting your flower bed?

The flower beds really don’t like when dogs pee on them but there’s nothing you can about it. There are owners that don’t allow their dogs to answer the call of nature onto the flower bed. But many don’t care. Don’t start unnecessary conflicts, because even dogs are part of the public space (the same goes for people who don’t agree with your activities). Therefore, leave the dogs a space in your street where they and their owners will be happy.

Try to grow the flower bed in another way. It helps if people can clearly see that it is a flower patch – for example, by planting flowering seedlings. Planting tall, solid plants such as amaranth has proven a good idea for us.

What plants can be planted on the street?

Don’t expect perennials to survive winter. Only something like mint can survive such difficult living conditions ;-) Otherwise, there are no limits to the seeds or seedlings you choose. We have a good experience with sowing marigolds, sunflowers, mallows, Indian cress and other common plants that don’t need pre-cultivation. We even managed to grow tomatoes, beans and pumpkins. If you buy seedlings, don’t invest much money in them, so that you don’t feel sorry when something goes wrong and that bitterness (which is inevitable and needs to be dealt with) doesn’t outweigh the joy of it. You can get discarded seedlings from a gardening store or a florist shop. With good watering, fertilization and sunlight, they can still work wonders. You can also use your own pre-grown seedlings. There is one more rule for seedlings and seeds – there must be enough of them. More than you expect to actually survive. Plants struggle on the streets.

What if my flower bed doesn’t survive winter?

Sowing in autumn is unfortunately not worth it. In winter, the flower beds get a serious beating from both humans and animals. Give them your time and energy (again) once spring arrives.

Try it and share it with us

Now you know everything about guerrilla gardening. Of course, your experience may be different from ours. We’ll be happy if you share them with us, for example on our Facebook page.

If you have any further questions, please contact us, we’ll be happy to share our experience with you. If you decide to become a guerrilla gardener, we’ll also be happy to welcome you among us.
